Study Guide

CONTRACTS LAW

Formation

Valid Contract

A legally binding agreement between parties
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual Assent (Offer and Acceptance)
  • Consideration

Offer

A manifestation of willingness to enter into a bargain, made to justify another person in understanding that assent is invited
Elements that must be proven:
  • Definite Terms (Q-Tips: Quantity, Time, Identity of parties, Price, Subject matter)
  • Communication to offeree

Termination of Offer

Circumstances that end an offer before acceptance
Methods of termination:
  • Lapse of time
  • Death or incapacity of offeror
  • Destruction of subject matter
  • Supervening illegality

Revocation

Offeror's withdrawal of offer before acceptance
Elements that must be proven:
  • Communication of revocation
  • Before acceptance
  • Effective when received

Rejection

Offeree's refusal to accept offer, terminating the offer
Elements that must be proven:
  • Clear refusal of offer
  • Communication to offeror
  • Counteroffer acts as rejection

Acceptance

A manifestation of assent to the terms of an offer
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mirror image rule compliance
  • Communication to offeror
  • Timely acceptance

Consideration

A bargained-for exchange; something of legal value given in exchange for a promise
Elements that must be proven:
  • Bargained-for exchange
  • Legal detriment or benefit

Defenses to Formation

Parol Evidence Rule

Rule excluding extrinsic evidence of prior or contemporaneous oral agreements that contradict a written contract

Statute of Frauds

Requirement that certain contracts must be in writing to be enforceable
Elements that must be proven:
  • Contract falls within statute (marriage, year, land, executor, goods over $500, surety)
  • Writing signed by party to be charged

Duress

Improper threat that induces a party to enter into a contract
Elements that must be proven:
  • Improper threat
  • No reasonable alternative
  • Induced the contract

Fraud

Intentional misrepresentation that induces reliance
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard
  • Intent to induce reliance
  • Justifiable reliance
  • Damages

Unconscionability

Contract terms that are so unfair as to shock the conscience
Elements that must be proven:
  • Procedural unconscionability (unfair bargaining process)
  • Substantive unconscionability (unfair terms)

Mistake

Erroneous belief about facts existing at time of contract formation
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mistake of material fact
  • Mistake existed at contract formation
  • Mutual mistake OR unilateral mistake known to other party

Negligent Misrepresentation

False statement made without reasonable care in commercial context
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Lack of reasonable care in making statement
  • Commercial/business context
  • Justifiable reliance

Incapacity

Lack of legal capacity to enter into binding contract
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental incapacity OR minority
  • Lack of understanding of nature and consequences
  • Other party knew or should have known

Illegality

Contract for illegal purpose or against public policy
Elements that must be proven:
  • Contract purpose violates law
  • Violation of public policy
  • Knowledge of illegality

Adhesion Contract

Standardized contract offered on take-it-or-leave-it basis
Elements that must be proven:
  • Standardized form contract
  • No opportunity to negotiate terms
  • Inequality of bargaining power

Ambiguity

Contract language susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation
Elements that must be proven:
  • Language reasonably susceptible to multiple meanings
  • Different interpretations by parties
  • Ambiguity affects material term

Third Party Rights

Third Party Beneficiary

A person who benefits from a contract between two other parties
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to benefit the third party
  • Third party is intended, not incidental beneficiary
  • Vesting of rights

Assignment

Transfer of contractual rights from assignor to assignee
Elements that must be proven:
  • Existing right capable of assignment
  • Clear intent to assign
  • Communication to assignee

Delegation

Transfer of contractual duties from delegator to delegatee
Elements that must be proven:
  • Delegable duty (not personal in nature)
  • Clear intent to delegate
  • Delegatee acceptance

Conditions to Performance

Condition vs. Covenant

Condition: event that must occur before duty to perform arises; Covenant: promise to perform
Elements that must be proven:
  • Language creating condition (if, unless, provided that, on condition that)
  • Event must occur before performance due

Condition Precedent

Condition that must be satisfied before contractual duty arises
Elements that must be proven:
  • Condition exists before duty to perform
  • Condition must be satisfied
  • Failure excuses other party's performance

Condition Concurrent

Conditions that are to be performed simultaneously by both parties
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual conditions
  • Simultaneous performance required
  • Tender of performance

Condition Subsequent

Condition that terminates existing contractual duty
Elements that must be proven:
  • Duty already exists
  • Condition operates to discharge duty
  • Condition occurs after formation

Excuse of Condition

Circumstances that excuse performance of condition
Elements that must be proven:
  • Prevention by other party
  • Waiver by benefited party
  • Estoppel
  • Disproportionate forfeiture

Discharge of Duty to Perform

Impossibility

Objective impossibility that makes performance impossible
Elements that must be proven:
  • Performance objectively impossible
  • Impossibility arose after contract formation
  • Not caused by breaching party

Impracticability

Performance possible but extremely burdensome due to unforeseen circumstances
Elements that must be proven:
  • Extreme and unreasonable difficulty
  • Unforeseeable circumstances
  • Not assumed by party

Frustration of Purpose

Principal purpose of contract substantially frustrated by supervening event
Elements that must be proven:
  • Principal purpose substantially frustrated
  • Frustration not reasonably foreseeable
  • Purpose known to both parties

Mutual Rescission

Agreement by both parties to cancel contract
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual agreement to rescind
  • Executory duties remain
  • Valid consideration for rescission

Substituted Contract

New contract that replaces and discharges original contract
Elements that must be proven:
  • New contract formed
  • Intent to substitute for original
  • Immediate discharge of original duties

Breach

Material Breach

A breach that goes to the essence of the contract
Elements that must be proven:
  • Failure to perform substantial part of contract
  • Defeats purpose of contract

Minor Breach

Breach that does not go to essence of contract but allows for damages

Remedies

Expectation Damages

Damages that put the injured party in the position they would have been in if the contract had been performed
Elements that must be proven:
  • Benefit of the bargain
  • Certainty of damages
  • Foreseeability
  • Mitigation

Reliance Damages

Damages that restore the injured party to the position before the contract

Restitution Damages

Damages that prevent unjust enrichment

Specific Performance

Equitable remedy requiring actual performance of contract terms
Elements that must be proven:
  • Inadequate remedy at law
  • Contract terms are definite
  • Consideration is adequate
  • No undue hardship

UCC Remedies

Buyer's Remedies

Remedies available to buyer when seller breaches sales contract
Remedies available:
  • Cover damages (difference between cover price and contract price)
  • Market damages (difference between market price and contract price)
  • Incidental and consequential damages
  • Right to reject or revoke acceptance

Seller's Remedies

Remedies available to seller when buyer breaches sales contract
Remedies available:
  • Resale damages (difference between contract price and resale price)
  • Market damages (difference between contract price and market price)
  • Lost profits (for lost volume seller)
  • Right to stop delivery or reclaim goods

CRIMINAL LAW

Formation of Crimes

Actus Reus

The guilty act; physical element of a crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary act or omission
  • Causation (actual and proximate)

Mens Rea

The guilty mind; mental element of a crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent (purposely, knowingly, recklessly, negligently)
  • Concurrence with actus reus

Accomplice Liability

Liability for aiding and abetting another's crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to aid the crime
  • Knowledge of the crime
  • Active assistance or encouragement

Vicarious Liability

Liability imposed on one person for acts of another based on relationship
Elements that must be proven:
  • Special relationship (employer-employee, principal-agent)
  • Act within scope of relationship
  • Criminal act by subordinate

Inchoate Crimes

Solicitation

Asking, encouraging, or commanding another to commit a crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent that target crime be committed
  • Communication of solicitation

Attempt

Intent to commit a crime plus substantial step toward commission
Elements that must be proven:
  • Specific intent to commit target crime
  • Substantial step beyond mere preparation

Conspiracy

Agreement between two or more persons to commit a crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Agreement between parties
  • Intent to commit target crime
  • Overt act (in most jurisdictions)

Crimes Against the Person

Homicide Analysis (IRAC Approach)

IRAC Homicide Method

Systematic approach to analyze homicide crimes
Step-by-step analysis:
  • 1. Causation (actual and proximate)
  • 2. Murder/Malice? (determine if malice exists)
  • 3. If YES - What degree? (First or Second degree)
  • 4. Justifications and Excuses
  • 5. Mitigation to Voluntary Manslaughter
  • 6. If NO malice - Involuntary Manslaughter

Murder

Unlawful killing of another human being with malice aforethought
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unlawful killing
  • Of another human being
  • With malice aforethought
  • Causation (actual and proximate)

First Degree Murder

Murder with premeditation and deliberation
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of murder
  • Premeditation
  • Deliberation

Voluntary Manslaughter

Intentional killing in heat of passion upon adequate provocation
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intentional killing
  • Adequate provocation
  • Heat of passion
  • No cooling off period

Involuntary Manslaughter

Unintentional killing through criminal negligence or during unlawful act
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unintentional killing
  • Criminal negligence or unlawful act
  • Causation

Assault

Intent to cause harmful/offensive contact or apprehension thereof
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause contact or apprehension
  • Victim's reasonable apprehension (for apprehension assault)

Battery

Unlawful application of force to another person
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause harmful/offensive contact
  • Harmful or offensive contact
  • Lack of consent

False Imprisonment

Unlawful restraint of person's freedom of movement
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to confine or restrain
  • Actual confinement or restraint
  • Against victim's will
  • Without legal justification

Kidnapping

Unlawful confinement with movement or concealment of victim
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unlawful confinement or restraint
  • Movement or concealment of victim
  • Against victim's will
  • Intent to confine

Mayhem

Malicious disfigurement or disabling of another person
Elements that must be proven:
  • Malicious intent
  • Disfigurement or disabling
  • Of another person
  • Permanent injury

Rape

Unlawful sexual intercourse by force or without consent
Elements that must be proven:
  • Sexual intercourse
  • By force or threat of force
  • Without consent
  • With another person

Crimes Against Habitation

Burglary

Breaking and entering the dwelling of another at night with intent to commit a felony
Elements that must be proven:
  • Breaking (modern law: any entry)
  • Entering
  • Dwelling of another
  • At night (traditional; not required in modern law)
  • Intent to commit felony inside

Arson

Malicious burning of the dwelling of another
Elements that must be proven:
  • Malicious burning
  • Of dwelling (or structure in modern law)
  • Of another

Crimes Against Property

Larceny

Trespassory taking and carrying away of personal property of another with intent to permanently deprive
Elements that must be proven:
  • Trespassory taking
  • Carrying away (asportation)
  • Personal property
  • Of another
  • Intent to permanently deprive

Robbery

Larceny by force or intimidation from the person or presence of the victim
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of larceny
  • Taking by force or intimidation
  • From person or presence of victim

Embezzlement

Fraudulent conversion of personal property by one in lawful possession
Elements that must be proven:
  • Lawful possession of property
  • Fraudulent conversion
  • Intent to permanently deprive

False Pretenses

Obtaining title to property by false representation of material fact
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Intent to defraud
  • Victim's reliance on representation
  • Obtaining title to property

Receiving Stolen Property

Knowingly receiving stolen property with intent to permanently deprive
Elements that must be proven:
  • Property was stolen
  • Defendant received/possessed property
  • Knowledge property was stolen
  • Intent to permanently deprive

Crimes Against Property Interests

Forgery

Making or altering written instrument with intent to defraud
Elements that must be proven:
  • Making or altering written instrument
  • Falsely purporting to be genuine
  • Instrument has apparent legal significance
  • Intent to defraud

Uttering

Offering or using forged instrument knowing it is false
Elements that must be proven:
  • Offering or using written instrument
  • Knowledge instrument is forged
  • Intent to defraud
  • Instrument purports to have legal significance

Extortion/Blackmail

Obtaining property by threats of future harm or exposure
Elements that must be proven:
  • Threat of future harm or exposure
  • Intent to obtain property
  • Victim's reasonable fear
  • Obtaining property through threat

Defenses

Self-Defense

Use of reasonable force to protect oneself from imminent unlawful force
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief in need for defense
  • Imminent threat
  • Proportionate force

Insanity

Mental disease or defect that negates criminal responsibility
Elements that must be proven (varies by test):
  • Mental disease or defect
  • Effect on understanding or control
  • At time of offense

Duress

Unlawful threat that coerces criminal conduct
Elements that must be proven:
  • Threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm
  • No reasonable means of escape
  • Did not place oneself in situation

Necessity

Choice of lesser evil when faced with emergency
Elements that must be proven:
  • Emergency situation
  • No legal alternative
  • Harm avoided greater than harm caused
  • Did not create emergency

Defense of Others

Use of reasonable force to protect third persons
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief third party in danger
  • Imminent threat to third party
  • Force proportionate to threat
  • Third party would be justified in self-defense

Defense of Property

Use of reasonable force to protect property
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief property threatened
  • Force necessary to protect property
  • No deadly force for property alone
  • Request to desist (when feasible)

Intoxication

Impairment from alcohol or drugs affecting mental state
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary or involuntary intoxication
  • Impairment of mental faculties
  • Effect on required mental state
  • Not for general intent crimes (voluntary)

Entrapment

Government inducement of crime by person not predisposed to commit it
Elements that must be proven:
  • Government inducement
  • Lack of predisposition to commit crime
  • Crime originated with government
  • Not mere opportunity

Mistake of Law

Ignorance or misunderstanding of the law (generally not a defense)
Elements (rarely successful):
  • Reasonable reliance on official interpretation
  • Law not reasonably available
  • Mistake negates specific intent element

Mistake of Fact

Misunderstanding of factual circumstances that negates mens rea
Elements that must be proven:
  • Honest mistake about facts
  • Mistake negates required mental state
  • Reasonable mistake (for negligence crimes)
  • Any mistake (for specific intent crimes)

TORTS LAW

Intentional Torts

Assault

Intent to cause harmful or offensive contact or imminent apprehension thereof
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause contact or apprehension
  • Reasonable apprehension of imminent contact
  • No consent

Battery

Intentional harmful or offensive contact with another person
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause harmful/offensive contact
  • Harmful or offensive contact
  • Lack of consent

False Imprisonment

Intentional confinement within boundaries fixed by actor
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to confine
  • Actual confinement
  • Awareness of confinement or harm
  • No reasonable means of escape

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Extreme and outrageous conduct intentionally causing severe emotional distress
Elements that must be proven:
  • Extreme and outrageous conduct
  • Intent or recklessness
  • Severe emotional distress
  • Causation

Trespass to Land

Intentional entry onto land in possession of another
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to enter land
  • Entry onto land
  • Land in possession of another
  • No consent or privilege

Conversion

Intentional interference with chattel so serious as to warrant payment of full value
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to exercise dominion/control
  • Serious interference with chattel
  • Chattel belongs to another

Trespass to Chattel

Intentional interference with another's chattel causing harm
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to interfere with chattel
  • Actual interference with chattel
  • Chattel belongs to another
  • Harm to chattel or possessor

Defenses to Intentional Torts

Consent

Voluntary agreement to the conduct that would otherwise constitute a tort
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary agreement
  • Capacity to consent
  • Knowledge of nature of act
  • No fraud or duress

Self-Defense

Use of reasonable force to protect oneself from harmful or offensive contact
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief in need for protection
  • Imminent threat
  • Force proportionate to threat
  • No excessive force

Defense of Others

Use of reasonable force to protect third persons from harm
Approaches:
  • Step-in-Shoes (limited to third party's right to self-defense)
  • Reasonable Appearances (based on defender's reasonable belief)

Defense of Property

Use of reasonable force to protect property from interference
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief property threatened
  • Request to desist (when feasible)
  • No deadly force for personal property
  • Force reasonably necessary

Necessity

Privileged interference with another's interests to avoid greater harm
Types:
  • Public Necessity (complete defense, no liability)
  • Private Necessity (limited defense, liable for actual harm)

Negligence

Negligence

Failure to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances
Elements that must be proven:
  • Duty of care
  • Breach of duty
  • Causation (actual and proximate)
  • Damages

Duty

Legal obligation to conform to standard of conduct for protection of others
General duty:
  • Foreseeability of harm
  • Relationship between parties
  • Policy considerations

Negligence Per Se

Violation of safety statute establishes breach of duty as matter of law
Elements that must be proven:
  • Violation of safety statute
  • Plaintiff in protected class
  • Harm of type statute designed to prevent
  • No excuse for violation

Special Duty Categories

Special relationships that create enhanced duties of care
Categories include:
  • Land Occupiers (invitees, licensees, trespassers)
  • Professionals (reasonable professional standard)
  • Common Carriers (highest degree of care)
  • Rescue/Good Samaritans (once undertaken, reasonable care)

Breach

Failure to meet the applicable standard of care
Elements that must be proven:
  • Standard of care (reasonable person)
  • Deviation from standard

Actual Causation

Factual connection between breach and harm
Elements that must be proven:
  • "But for" causation OR
  • Substantial factor test

Proximate Causation

Legal limitation on liability based on foreseeability
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeability of harm
  • Direct causation OR
  • No superseding intervening cause

Res Ipsa Loquitur

Doctrine allowing inference of negligence from circumstances
Elements that must be proven:
  • Event ordinarily doesn't occur without negligence
  • Instrumentality under defendant's exclusive control
  • No contributory negligence by plaintiff

Negligence Defenses

Contributory Negligence

Plaintiff's failure to exercise reasonable care contributing to harm (complete bar)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Plaintiff's unreasonable conduct
  • Contributing cause to harm
  • Failure to protect own safety

Comparative Negligence

Damages reduced by percentage of plaintiff's fault
Types:
  • Pure Comparative (damages reduced by any % fault)
  • Modified Comparative (no recovery if plaintiff >50% at fault)

Last Clear Chance

Defendant had last clear chance to avoid harm despite plaintiff's negligence
Elements that must be proven:
  • Plaintiff in position of helpless peril
  • Defendant aware of plaintiff's peril
  • Defendant had opportunity to avoid harm
  • Defendant failed to use reasonable care

Assumption of Risk

Plaintiff voluntarily assumed known risk of harm
Elements that must be proven:
  • Knowledge of specific risk
  • Voluntary assumption of risk
  • Risk materialized and caused harm

Strict Liability

Strict Liability

Liability without fault for certain activities or products
Elements that must be proven:
  • Abnormally dangerous activity OR defective product
  • Causation
  • Damages
  • Within scope of risk

Products Liability

Manufacturing Defect

Product differs from manufacturer's intended design
Elements that must be proven:
  • Product differs from intended design
  • Defect existed when product left defendant
  • Defect caused plaintiff's injury
  • Defendant in commercial chain of distribution

Design Defect

All products of same design are dangerous due to poor design
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeable risks of harm
  • Risk could have been reduced by reasonable alternative design
  • Alternative design's benefits outweigh costs
  • Design defect caused harm

Warning Defect

Failure to provide adequate warnings or instructions
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeable risks of harm
  • Risk could be reduced by warnings/instructions
  • Failure to provide adequate warnings
  • Lack of warning caused harm

Vicarious Liability

Respondeat Superior

Employer liability for employee's torts within scope of employment
Elements that must be proven:
  • Employer-employee relationship
  • Employee acting within scope of employment
  • Tort committed by employee
  • Tort occurred during work time/place

Independent Contractor

Generally no vicarious liability for independent contractor's torts
Exceptions (when liability exists):
  • Non-delegable duty
  • Inherently dangerous activity
  • Apparent authority
  • Negligent selection/supervision

Joint Enterprise

Mutual right of control creates vicarious liability among participants
Elements that must be proven:
  • Agreement to participate in common purpose
  • Common pecuniary interest
  • Equal right of control
  • Tort by one participant

Automobile Owner Liability

Owner liability for permissive use of vehicle
Elements that must be proven:
  • Owner gave permission to use vehicle
  • Use within scope of permission
  • Negligent driving by permittee
  • Causal connection to harm

Business Torts

Interference with Contract

Intentional interference with existing contractual relationship
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid contract between plaintiff and third party
  • Defendant's knowledge of contract
  • Intentional interference
  • Breach of contract
  • Damages to plaintiff

Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

Intentional interference with reasonably probable future economic benefit
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonably probable future economic benefit
  • Defendant's knowledge of relationship
  • Intentional interference
  • Loss of prospective benefit
  • Wrongful means or lack of justification

Defamation

Defamation

False statement of fact that harms reputation
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement of fact
  • Publication to third party
  • Damages to reputation
  • Fault (negligence for private figures, actual malice for public figures)

Libel

Written defamation

Slander

Spoken defamation

Invasion of Privacy

Intrusion Upon Seclusion

Intentional intrusion into private affairs that would be highly offensive
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intentional intrusion
  • Private place/matter
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person

Public Disclosure of Private Facts

Publication of private facts offensive and not of legitimate public concern
Elements that must be proven:
  • Publication of private facts
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person
  • Not of legitimate public concern

Appropriation of Likeness

Use of plaintiff's name or likeness for commercial benefit without consent
Elements that must be proven:
  • Use of plaintiff's name or likeness
  • For commercial purposes
  • Without plaintiff's consent
  • Damages to plaintiff

False Light

Publication placing plaintiff in false light highly offensive to reasonable person
Elements that must be proven:
  • Publication of material concerning plaintiff
  • Places plaintiff in false light
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person
  • Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard

Damages

Special Damages

Out-of-pocket economic losses

General Damages

Pain and suffering, emotional distress

Punitive Damages

Damages to punish and deter, awarded for willful/malicious conduct
Elements that must be proven:
  • Willful, wanton, or malicious conduct
  • Clear and convincing evidence

Miscellaneous Tort Concepts

Wrongful Death

Statutory cause of action for death caused by defendant's tort
Elements that must be proven:
  • Death of person
  • Caused by defendant's tort
  • Statutory beneficiary bringing suit
  • Pecuniary loss to beneficiaries

Survival Action

Deceased person's tort claim survives death for estate's benefit
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid tort claim by decedent
  • Claim accrued before death
  • Proper representative bringing suit
  • Damages from tort to decedent

Nuisance

Substantial and unreasonable interference with use and enjoyment of land
Elements that must be proven:
  • Substantial interference
  • Unreasonable interference
  • With use and enjoyment of land
  • Causation by defendant's conduct
