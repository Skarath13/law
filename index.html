Study Guide

CONTRACTS LAW

Formation

Governing Law

Whether common law or UCC applies - UCC covers sales of goods, common law covers everything else like services and real estate
Key distinctions:
  • UCC Article 2 applies to sales of goods
  • Common law applies to services, real estate, employment
  • Mixed contracts use predominant purpose test

Valid Contract

A deal that courts will enforce - basically when two people agree to something and the law says "yep, you both have to do what you promised"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual Assent (Offer and Acceptance)
  • Consideration

Offer

When someone says "I'll do X if you do Y" with enough detail that the other person can say yes and make a binding deal
Elements that must be proven:
  • Definite Terms (Q-Tips: Quantity, Time, Identity of parties, Price, Subject matter)
  • Communication to offeree

Termination of Offer

Ways an offer can die before anyone accepts it - like expiring, or the person who made it dies, or it becomes illegal
Methods of termination:
  • Lapse of time
  • Death or incapacity of offeror
  • Destruction of subject matter
  • Supervening illegality

Revocation

When the person who made an offer changes their mind and takes it back before the other person says yes
Elements that must be proven:
  • Communication of revocation
  • Before acceptance
  • Effective when received

Rejection

When someone says "no thanks" to an offer - once you reject it, you can't change your mind and accept it later
Elements that must be proven:
  • Clear refusal of offer
  • Communication to offeror
  • Counteroffer acts as rejection

Acceptance

Saying "yes, I agree" to someone's offer - but it has to match exactly what they offered (no changes allowed)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mirror image rule compliance
  • Communication to offeror
  • Timely acceptance

Consideration

Both sides have to give up something of value - you can't have a one-sided deal where only one person benefits
Elements that must be proven:
  • Bargained-for exchange
  • Legal detriment or benefit

Defenses to Formation

Parol Evidence Rule

If you put your agreement in writing, you generally can't bring up side conversations or promises that contradict what's written down

Statute of Frauds

Some important deals (like buying land or agreements taking over a year) must be written down or courts won't enforce them
Elements that must be proven:
  • Contract falls within statute (marriage, year, land, executor, goods over $500, surety)
  • Writing signed by party to be charged

Duress

When someone forces you into a deal by threatening you - like "sign this or I'll hurt you"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Improper threat
  • No reasonable alternative
  • Induced the contract

Fraud

When someone lies to you on purpose to get you to agree to a deal, and you believe the lie
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard
  • Intent to induce reliance
  • Justifiable reliance
  • Damages

Unconscionability

A deal so ridiculously unfair that it makes judges go "wait, what?" - like charging $10,000 for a candy bar
Elements that must be proven:
  • Procedural unconscionability (unfair bargaining process)
  • Substantive unconscionability (unfair terms)

Mistake

When both people (or just one person that the other knew was confused) were wrong about important facts when making the deal
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mistake of material fact
  • Mistake existed at contract formation
  • Mutual mistake OR unilateral mistake known to other party

Negligent Misrepresentation

When someone in business tells you something false because they were careless about checking their facts
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Lack of reasonable care in making statement
  • Commercial/business context
  • Justifiable reliance

Incapacity

When someone can't legally make contracts - like kids, mentally ill people, or extremely drunk people
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental incapacity OR minority
  • Lack of understanding of nature and consequences
  • Other party knew or should have known

Illegality

Deals to do illegal stuff - courts won't help you collect money for selling drugs or hiring a hitman
Elements that must be proven:
  • Contract purpose violates law
  • Violation of public policy
  • Knowledge of illegality

Adhesion Contract

Those "take it or leave it" contracts with no negotiation - like software licenses or insurance policies
Elements that must be proven:
  • Standardized form contract
  • No opportunity to negotiate terms
  • Inequality of bargaining power

Ambiguity

When contract language is so unclear that reasonable people could understand it different ways
Elements that must be proven:
  • Language reasonably susceptible to multiple meanings
  • Different interpretations by parties
  • Ambiguity affects material term

Third Party Rights

Third Party Beneficiary (Mnemonic: Does Public Intercourse Cause VD?)

Someone who gets benefits from a deal they weren't part of making - like when your parents hire someone to pay your college tuition

Define Third Party Beneficiary

A person who is not a party to the contract but who benefits from it and may have rights to enforce it

Privity

Third party beneficiaries can sue even without privity of contract - they get rights through the original parties' intent
Elements that must be proven:
  • No direct contractual relationship required
  • Rights flow from original contract

Intent to Benefit Third Party

The contracting parties must have specifically intended to benefit the third party, not just accidentally help them
Elements that must be proven:
  • Express intent to benefit third party
  • Performance would satisfy obligation to third party
  • Third party identified in contract

Classification of Third Party Beneficiaries

Third party beneficiaries are either "intended" (can sue) or "incidental" (cannot sue) - only intended beneficiaries have rights
Types:
  • Intended Beneficiary - specifically intended to benefit, can sue
  • Incidental Beneficiary - accidentally benefits, cannot sue
  • Creditor Beneficiary - performance pays a debt owed to beneficiary
  • Donee Beneficiary - performance is a gift to beneficiary

Vesting of Third Party Rights

When a third party's rights become permanent and can't be taken away by the original parties changing their minds
Rights vest when:
  • Third party learns of and assents to contract
  • Third party relies on the contract
  • Third party brings lawsuit

Divide Lawsuits if Necessary

Third party beneficiaries may need to bring separate suits against each original party depending on who breached
Lawsuit considerations:
  • Sue promisor for non-performance
  • Original parties can still sue each other
  • Defenses available against third party

Assignment

Giving away your right to get something from a contract - like selling your lottery ticket to someone else
Elements that must be proven:
  • Existing right capable of assignment
  • Clear intent to assign
  • Communication to assignee

Delegation

Having someone else do your job under a contract - but you're still responsible if they mess up
Elements that must be proven:
  • Delegable duty (not personal in nature)
  • Clear intent to delegate
  • Delegatee acceptance

Conditions to Performance

Condition vs. Covenant

Condition: something that must happen before you have to do your part. Covenant: a promise you must keep regardless
Elements that must be proven:
  • Language creating condition (if, unless, provided that, on condition that)
  • Event must occur before performance due

Condition Precedent

Something that must happen first before anyone has to perform - like "I'll pay you IF you pass the test"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Condition exists before duty to perform
  • Condition must be satisfied
  • Failure excuses other party's performance

Condition Concurrent

Both people have to do their part at the same time - like handing over keys while receiving payment for a car
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual conditions
  • Simultaneous performance required
  • Tender of performance

Condition Subsequent

Something that cancels out duties that already exist - like "you don't have to pay IF I get fired"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Duty already exists
  • Condition operates to discharge duty
  • Condition occurs after formation

Express Conditions

Conditions clearly stated in the contract using words like "if," "unless," "provided that," or "on condition that"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Clear conditional language
  • Specific event or occurrence
  • Must be satisfied exactly

Implied Conditions

Conditions not expressly stated but understood from circumstances or imposed by law

Implied in Fact Conditions

Conditions that the parties intended but didn't explicitly state - inferred from their conduct and circumstances
Elements that must be proven:
  • Party conduct indicates conditional intent
  • Circumstances support implication
  • Reasonable person would infer condition

Implied in Law Conditions

Conditions imposed by courts as a matter of law for fairness - like constructive conditions of exchange
Elements that must be proven:
  • Legal policy supports implication
  • Fairness requires condition
  • Prevents unjust enrichment

Were Plaintiff's Conditions Satisfied?

Analysis of whether the defendant performed well enough to trigger the plaintiff's duty to perform

Substantial Performance (Constructive Conditions)

When someone does almost all of what they promised, minor deviations don't excuse the other party's performance
Elements that must be proven:
  • Performance of essential purposes
  • Only minor deviations
  • Good faith effort
  • Damages can compensate for deviations

Divisibility

When a contract can be broken into separate parts, substantial performance of one part may require payment for that part
Elements that must be proven:
  • Contract capable of division
  • Separate consideration for each part
  • Performance of divisible part substantially complete

Were Plaintiff's Conditions Excused?

Reasons why someone doesn't have to meet a condition - like if the other person prevented it from happening

Waiver

Voluntarily giving up a condition - like accepting late performance without complaining
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary relinquishment of condition
  • Knowledge of condition
  • Clear intent to waive

Anticipatory Repudiation

When someone clearly indicates they won't perform before performance is due - excuses conditions and allows immediate suit
Elements that must be proven:
  • Clear and unequivocal repudiation
  • Before performance due
  • Goes to essence of contract

Prevention

When one party prevents the other from satisfying a condition - the condition is excused
Elements that must be proven:
  • Affirmative act preventing performance
  • Intent to prevent or reckless disregard
  • Prevention of condition satisfaction

Impossibility of Performance

When it becomes literally impossible to satisfy a condition through no fault of either party
Elements that must be proven:
  • Objective impossibility
  • Not caused by party seeking excuse
  • Supervening event

Voluntary Disablement

When a party voluntarily makes it impossible for them to perform their obligations
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary act by obligor
  • Makes performance impossible
  • Repudiation through conduct

Estoppel

When someone acts in a way that makes it unfair to enforce a condition against them
Elements that must be proven:
  • Misrepresentation or misleading conduct
  • Reasonable reliance by other party
  • Injustice if condition enforced

Discharge of Duty to Perform

Merger

When a contract is replaced by a judgment, deed, or other formal legal document - the original contract duties merge into the new document
Elements that must be proven:
  • Formal legal document created
  • Document embodies contract terms
  • Intent to merge obligations

Modification

Changing the terms of an existing contract - both parties must agree and provide consideration (unless UCC)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual agreement to modify
  • Consideration for modification (common law)
  • Good faith (UCC)

Amendment and Restatement of Contract

Formally rewriting the contract with new terms while keeping the essential agreement intact
Elements that must be proven:
  • Written amendment or restatement
  • Mutual agreement to changes
  • Core contractual relationship continues

Novation

Replacing one party to the contract with a new party - completely releases the original party from obligations
Elements that must be proven:
  • New party substituted
  • All parties agree to substitution
  • Intent to release original obligor

Accord and Satisfaction

Agreement to accept different performance to settle a dispute - once new performance is completed, original duty is discharged
Elements that must be proven:
  • Disputed or unliquidated claim
  • Agreement to accept substitute performance (accord)
  • Performance of substitute (satisfaction)

Release

Voluntary giving up of a claim or right - usually must be in writing and supported by consideration
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary relinquishment
  • Clear intent to release
  • Consideration or executed gift

Waiver

Voluntarily giving up a right or condition - can be express or implied from conduct
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary relinquishment
  • Knowledge of right being waived
  • Express statement or conduct showing intent

Supervening Illegality

When performance becomes illegal after the contract is formed due to new laws or regulations
Elements that must be proven:
  • Law change after contract formation
  • Performance now violates law
  • Not caused by breaching party

Failure of Consideration

When the reason for making the contract completely fails - like paying for a concert that gets permanently cancelled
Elements that must be proven:
  • Total failure of consideration
  • No partial benefit received
  • Failure not caused by plaintiff

Impossibility

When it becomes literally impossible for anyone to do what was promised - like the building you were supposed to paint burns down
Elements that must be proven:
  • Performance objectively impossible
  • Impossibility arose after contract formation
  • Not caused by breaching party

Impracticability

When you technically could still do what you promised, but it would be so crazy expensive or difficult that it's basically impossible
Elements that must be proven:
  • Extreme and unreasonable difficulty
  • Unforeseeable circumstances
  • Not assumed by party

Frustration of Purpose

When the whole reason for making the deal becomes pointless due to circumstances - like renting a room to watch a parade that gets cancelled
Elements that must be proven:
  • Principal purpose substantially frustrated
  • Frustration not reasonably foreseeable
  • Purpose known to both parties

Commercial or Economic Impracticability

Performance becomes extremely burdensome due to unforeseeable circumstances, making it unreasonably expensive or difficult - like requiring 10 times more cost than expected
Elements that must be proven:
  • Performance has become impracticable
  • Due to occurrence of unforeseen event
  • Non-occurrence was basic assumption of contract
  • Party seeking discharge did not assume risk

Mutual Rescission

Both people agree to call off the deal and pretend it never happened
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mutual agreement to rescind
  • Executory duties remain
  • Valid consideration for rescission

Substituted Contract

Making a completely new deal that replaces the old one - the old deal is gone forever
Elements that must be proven:
  • New contract formed
  • Intent to substitute for original
  • Immediate discharge of original duties

Breach

Material Breach

Breaking the deal so badly that it defeats the whole point - the other person can walk away and sue you
Elements that must be proven:
  • Failure to perform substantial part of contract
  • Defeats purpose of contract

Minor Breach

Breaking some part of the deal, but not so bad that it ruins everything - you still have to perform, but can get some money back

Remedies

Legal vs. Equitable Remedies (Mnemonic: Lively Cats Find Mice Darn Nice)

Legal remedies are money damages; equitable remedies are court orders to do or not do something - you must try legal remedies first
Legal Remedies:
  • Compensatory damages
  • Consequential damages
  • Incidental damages
  • Liquidated damages
Equitable Remedies:
  • Specific performance
  • Injunction
  • Rescission
  • Reformation

General Damages

Damages that naturally and obviously flow from the breach - like not getting the thing you paid for
Elements that must be proven:
  • Natural consequence of breach
  • Foreseeable at contract formation
  • Direct result of breach

Special Damages

Unusual damages that don't automatically flow from breach - must be specifically foreseeable to the breaching party
Elements that must be proven:
  • Arising from special circumstances
  • Communicated to or known by breaching party
  • Foreseeable consequence
  • Certainty of amount

Liquidated Damages

Pre-agreed amount of damages written into the contract - enforceable if reasonable, not if it's a penalty
Elements that must be proven:
  • Difficulty in calculating actual damages
  • Reasonable estimate at time of contract
  • Not a penalty

Punitive Damages (NOT allowed in contracts)

Generally not available in contract cases - contracts are about compensation, not punishment
Exceptions (rare):
  • Breach involves independent tort
  • Bad faith by insurance company
  • Willful breach with malicious intent

Nominal Damages

Small amount of money (like $1) awarded when there's a breach but no actual harm - recognizes legal rights were violated
Elements that must be proven:
  • Breach of contract occurred
  • No actual damages suffered
  • Legal rights were violated

Avoidable Consequence Rule (Mitigation)

You can't collect damages you could have reasonably prevented - like finding another job after wrongful termination
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable opportunity to mitigate
  • Mitigation was possible
  • Failure to make reasonable efforts

Collateral Source Rule

Benefits received from other sources (like insurance) don't reduce damages owed by the breaching party
Elements that must be proven:
  • Benefit from independent source
  • Source not connected to breaching party
  • Policy supports non-reduction

Quasi-Contract (Restitution)

When no valid contract exists but one person got unjustly enriched - court orders payment of reasonable value of benefits
Elements that must be proven:
  • Benefit conferred on defendant
  • Defendant has knowledge of benefit
  • Unjust for defendant to retain benefit
  • No valid contract exists

Rescission

Canceling the contract and putting both parties back where they started - like it never happened
Elements that must be proven:
  • Material breach or grounds for rescission
  • Prompt action after discovering grounds
  • Restoration possible

Reformation

Court rewrites the contract to reflect what the parties actually intended - usually due to mistake or fraud
Elements that must be proven:
  • Written contract doesn't reflect true agreement
  • Mutual mistake or fraud
  • Clear evidence of true intent

Injunction

Court order requiring someone to do something or stop doing something - for ongoing or threatened breaches
Elements that must be proven:
  • Inadequate remedy at law
  • Irreparable harm
  • Balancing of hardships favors plaintiff
  • Feasible enforcement

Expectation Damages

Money to put you in the position you'd be in if the other person had kept their promise - like the profit you would have made
Elements that must be proven:
  • Benefit of the bargain
  • Certainty of damages
  • Foreseeability
  • Mitigation

Reliance Damages

Money to cover what you spent getting ready for a deal that fell through - like reimbursing your moving expenses

Restitution Damages

Money to prevent someone from getting rich off you unfairly - making them give back what they shouldn't have

Specific Performance (Mnemonic: Charles Atlas Earns Much Cash Delivering Elephants)

When a judge orders someone to actually do what they promised instead of just paying money - usually for unique items like houses
Elements that must be proven:
  • Inadequate remedy at law
  • Contract terms are definite
  • Consideration is adequate
  • No undue hardship

UCC Remedies

Buyer's Remedies

What you can do when someone doesn't deliver goods you paid for - like buying replacement goods and making them pay the difference
Remedies available:
  • Cover damages (difference between cover price and contract price)
  • Market damages (difference between market price and contract price)
  • Incidental and consequential damages
  • Right to reject or revoke acceptance

Seller's Remedies

What you can do when someone doesn't pay for goods you delivered - like selling to someone else and making them cover any loss
Remedies available:
  • Resale damages (difference between contract price and resale price)
  • Market damages (difference between contract price and market price)
  • Lost profits (for lost volume seller)
  • Right to stop delivery or reclaim goods

CRIMINAL LAW

Formation of Crimes

Actus Reus

The bad thing you actually did - the physical action part of a crime (you can't just think about doing bad things)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary act or omission
  • Causation (actual and proximate)

Mens Rea

Your mental state when you did the bad thing - did you mean to do it, were you reckless, or just careless?
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent (purposely, knowingly, recklessly, negligently)
  • Concurrence with actus reus

Accomplice Liability

Getting in trouble for helping someone else commit a crime - like being the getaway driver for a bank robbery
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to aid the crime
  • Knowledge of the crime
  • Active assistance or encouragement

Vicarious Liability

When you're responsible for crimes committed by people working for you - like a boss being liable for employee actions
Elements that must be proven:
  • Special relationship (employer-employee, principal-agent)
  • Act within scope of relationship
  • Criminal act by subordinate

Inchoate Crimes

Solicitation

Trying to get someone else to commit a crime - like asking your friend to rob a store for you
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent that target crime be committed
  • Communication of solicitation

Attempt

Trying to commit a crime but not finishing it - like breaking into a house but running away when the alarm goes off
Elements that must be proven:
  • Specific intent to commit target crime
  • Substantial step beyond mere preparation

Conspiracy

Planning a crime with other people - you can get in trouble just for making the plan, even if you never do the crime
Elements that must be proven:
  • Agreement between parties
  • Intent to commit target crime
  • Overt act (in most jurisdictions)

Crimes Against the Person

Homicide Analysis (IRAC Approach)

IRAC Homicide Method

A step-by-step way to figure out what level of killing charge someone gets - from murder down to manslaughter
Step-by-step analysis:
  • 1. Causation (actual and proximate)
  • 2. Murder/Malice? (determine if malice exists)
  • 3. If YES - What degree? (First or Second degree)
  • 4. Justifications and Excuses
  • 5. Mitigation to Voluntary Manslaughter
  • 6. If NO malice - Involuntary Manslaughter

Causation

Proving that the defendant's actions actually caused the victim's death - both factually and legally

Actual Cause

Did the defendant's actions actually bring about the death? Would the victim still be alive "but for" the defendant's actions?
Elements that must be proven:
  • "But for" defendant's act, victim would not have died
  • Defendant's act was substantial factor in death
  • Death occurred within a year and a day (traditional rule)

Proximate Cause

Even if defendant actually caused death, they're only responsible if the death was a foreseeable result of their actions
Elements that must be proven:
  • Death was natural and probable consequence
  • No unforeseeable intervening causes
  • Victim taken as found (eggshell skull rule)
  • Medical malpractice generally doesn't break chain

Murder

Killing someone on purpose, or doing something so dangerous you knew someone would probably die
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unlawful killing
  • Of another human being
  • With malice aforethought
  • Causation (actual and proximate)

First Degree Murder

The worst kind of murder - when you planned it out ahead of time and thought about it before doing it
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of murder
  • Premeditation
  • Deliberation

Second Degree Murder

Murder with malice but without premeditation and deliberation - acting with intent to kill or extreme recklessness but in the heat of the moment
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of murder
  • Malice aforethought
  • No premeditation or deliberation
  • Intent to kill OR extreme recklessness

Voluntary Manslaughter

Killing someone on purpose, but you were so angry or upset that you couldn't think straight - like catching your spouse cheating
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intentional killing
  • Adequate provocation
  • Heat of passion
  • No cooling off period

Involuntary Manslaughter

Accidentally killing someone because you were being extremely careless or doing something illegal (but not trying to kill)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unintentional killing
  • Criminal negligence or unlawful act
  • Causation

Assault

Either trying to hurt someone or making them think you're about to hurt them - like swinging a punch or threatening someone
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause contact or apprehension
  • Victim's reasonable apprehension (for apprehension assault)

Battery

Actually touching someone in a harmful or offensive way without permission - from a punch to an unwanted kiss
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause harmful/offensive contact
  • Harmful or offensive contact
  • Lack of consent

False Imprisonment

Keeping someone trapped against their will - like locking them in a room or blocking their car in a parking lot
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to confine or restrain
  • Actual confinement or restraint
  • Against victim's will
  • Without legal justification

Kidnapping

Taking someone against their will and moving them or hiding them somewhere - more serious than false imprisonment
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unlawful confinement or restraint
  • Movement or concealment of victim
  • Against victim's will
  • Intent to confine

Mayhem

Permanently hurting someone on purpose - like cutting off a finger or blinding someone
Elements that must be proven:
  • Malicious intent
  • Disfigurement or disabling
  • Of another person
  • Permanent injury

Rape

Forcing someone to have sex or having sex with someone who can't legally consent
Elements that must be proven:
  • Sexual intercourse
  • By force or threat of force
  • Without consent
  • With another person

Crimes Against Habitation

Burglary

Breaking into someone's house (or any building) planning to commit a crime once you're inside
Elements that must be proven:
  • Breaking (modern law: any entry)
  • Entering
  • Dwelling of another
  • At night (traditional; not required in modern law)
  • Intent to commit felony inside

Arson

Intentionally setting fire to someone else's building - one of the most serious property crimes
Elements that must be proven:
  • Malicious burning
  • Of dwelling (or structure in modern law)
  • Of another

Crimes Against Property

Larceny

Stealing someone else's stuff by taking it without permission and planning to keep it forever - basic theft
Elements that must be proven:
  • Trespassory taking
  • Carrying away (asportation)
  • Personal property
  • Of another
  • Intent to permanently deprive

Larceny by Trick

Getting someone to give you their property by lying, but they only intended to give you temporary possession - like borrowing a car and never returning it
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of larceny
  • Victim consents to possession (not ownership)
  • Consent obtained by fraud or deception
  • Intent to permanently deprive at time of taking

Robbery

Stealing something directly from a person using force or threats - like a mugging or sticking up a store
Elements that must be proven:
  • All elements of larceny
  • Taking by force or intimidation
  • From person or presence of victim

Embezzlement

Stealing money or property that was legally given to you to hold - like a cashier taking money from the register
Elements that must be proven:
  • Lawful possession of property
  • Fraudulent conversion
  • Intent to permanently deprive

False Pretenses

Getting someone to give you their stuff by lying to them about important facts - like selling fake jewelry as real diamonds
Elements that must be proven:
  • False representation of material fact
  • Intent to defraud
  • Victim's reliance on representation
  • Obtaining title to property

Receiving Stolen Property

Taking or buying stuff that you know was stolen - like buying a "hot" TV from someone's trunk
Elements that must be proven:
  • Property was stolen
  • Defendant received/possessed property
  • Knowledge property was stolen
  • Intent to permanently deprive

Crimes Against Property Interests

Forgery

Creating or changing important documents to trick people - like faking a signature on a check
Elements that must be proven:
  • Making or altering written instrument
  • Falsely purporting to be genuine
  • Instrument has apparent legal significance
  • Intent to defraud

Uttering

Using a fake document that you know is fake - like trying to cash a forged check
Elements that must be proven:
  • Offering or using written instrument
  • Knowledge instrument is forged
  • Intent to defraud
  • Instrument purports to have legal significance

Extortion/Blackmail

Getting money or stuff from someone by threatening to hurt them or reveal their secrets if they don't pay
Elements that must be proven:
  • Threat of future harm or exposure
  • Intent to obtain property
  • Victim's reasonable fear
  • Obtaining property through threat

Miscellaneous Crimes

Misprision

Knowing about a federal felony and not reporting it while also taking some step to conceal it - like hiding evidence
Elements that must be proven:
  • Knowledge of commission of federal felony
  • Failure to report to authorities
  • Affirmative step to conceal crime

Compounding

Agreeing not to prosecute a crime in exchange for money or other consideration - like taking a bribe to not call the cops
Elements that must be proven:
  • Knowledge of crime
  • Agreement not to prosecute
  • Receipt of consideration

Riot

Violent disturbance by a group of people acting together - usually requires minimum number of people (like 3 or 5)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Group of people (minimum number varies)
  • Acting together
  • Using or threatening violence
  • Disturbing the peace

Rout

When people gather with intent to riot but haven't actually started the violence yet - preparation stage before riot
Elements that must be proven:
  • Assembly of people
  • Intent to commit riot
  • Some act toward carrying out intent

Unlawful Assembly

Group of people gathering for an illegal purpose or in a way likely to cause public alarm - before any violence
Elements that must be proven:
  • Assembly of people
  • Illegal purpose or alarming manner
  • Intent to carry out purpose

Malicious Mischief

Intentionally damaging or destroying someone else's property out of spite or just to cause trouble
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intentional damage to property
  • Property belongs to another
  • Malicious intent

Breach of Peace

Disturbing public order and tranquility - catch-all crime for disruptive behavior that upsets the community
Elements that must be proven:
  • Public disturbance
  • Likely to provoke violence or alarm
  • No lawful justification

Defenses: Justifications and Excuses

Justification Defenses (crime committed, but allowed)

Defenses where the person did commit the crime but society says it was justified under the circumstances

Self-Defense

Fighting back to protect yourself when someone is attacking you - but only using as much force as necessary
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief in need for defense
  • Imminent threat
  • Proportionate force

Insanity (5 Rules)

Being so mentally ill that you can't understand right from wrong or control your actions - very hard to prove

1. M'Naghten Rule (Right vs. Wrong Test)

Defendant didn't know the nature and quality of their act OR didn't know it was wrong
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental disease or defect
  • Didn't know nature and quality of act OR
  • Didn't know act was wrong

2. Durham Rule (Product Test)

Criminal act was the product of mental disease or defect - rarely used today
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental disease or defect
  • Criminal act was product of the disease

3. ALI/MPC Test (Model Penal Code)

Combines cognitive and volitional prongs - lack of substantial capacity to appreciate wrongfulness or conform conduct
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental disease or defect
  • Lacks substantial capacity to appreciate criminality OR
  • Lacks substantial capacity to conform conduct to law

4. Irresistible Impulse Test

Defendant knew right from wrong but couldn't control their actions due to mental disease
Elements that must be proven:
  • Mental disease or defect
  • Unable to control conduct
  • Loss of power to choose

5. Federal/Hinckley Test

Narrowest test - defendant unable to appreciate nature, quality, or wrongfulness of acts (eliminates volitional prong)
Elements that must be proven:
  • Severe mental disease or defect
  • Unable to appreciate nature and quality of act
  • Unable to appreciate wrongfulness

Duress

Being forced to commit a crime because someone threatened to kill or seriously hurt you or your family
Elements that must be proven:
  • Threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm
  • No reasonable means of escape
  • Did not place oneself in situation

Necessity

Breaking the law to prevent something much worse from happening - like speeding to get someone to the hospital
Elements that must be proven:
  • Emergency situation
  • No legal alternative
  • Harm avoided greater than harm caused
  • Did not create emergency

Defense of Others

Fighting to protect someone else who's being attacked - same rules as self-defense but for other people
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief third party in danger
  • Imminent threat to third party
  • Force proportionate to threat
  • Third party would be justified in self-defense

Prevention of Crime

Using reasonable force to stop someone from committing a crime - more force allowed for felonies than misdemeanors
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief crime being committed
  • Force necessary to prevent crime
  • Force proportionate to crime threatened

Legal Authority

Acting under valid legal authority - like police making arrests or parents disciplining children

Domestic Authority

Parents, guardians, and teachers can use reasonable force to discipline children under their care
Elements that must be proven:
  • Authority relationship exists
  • Reasonable force used
  • For proper purpose (discipline, safety)

Public Authority

Police officers and other officials acting within the scope of their lawful authority
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid law enforcement authority
  • Acting within scope of duties
  • Reasonable force under circumstances

Consent

When the victim agrees to the conduct - like in contact sports or medical procedures
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary consent
  • Capacity to consent
  • Consent covers actual conduct
  • No fraud or duress

Excuse Defenses (no crime due to no mens rea)

Defenses where the person did the act but didn't have the mental state required for criminal liability

Infancy

Very young children cannot be held criminally responsible - different age limits in different jurisdictions
Common age categories:
  • Under 7 - no criminal liability
  • 7-14 - rebuttable presumption of no liability
  • 14-18 - juvenile court jurisdiction
  • Over 18 - full adult liability

Defense of Property

Using force to protect your stuff - but you can't kill someone just to protect property
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief property threatened
  • Force necessary to protect property
  • No deadly force for property alone
  • Request to desist (when feasible)

Intoxication

Being so drunk or high that you couldn't form the specific intent required for certain crimes
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary or involuntary intoxication
  • Impairment of mental faculties
  • Effect on required mental state
  • Not for general intent crimes (voluntary)

Entrapment

When police trick you into committing a crime you wouldn't normally do - they can't create criminals, just catch them
Elements that must be proven:
  • Government inducement
  • Lack of predisposition to commit crime
  • Crime originated with government
  • Not mere opportunity

Mistake of Law

Not knowing something was illegal - usually doesn't help you because "ignorance of the law is no excuse"
Elements (rarely successful):
  • Reasonable reliance on official interpretation
  • Law not reasonably available
  • Mistake negates specific intent element

Mistake of Fact

Being wrong about the facts in a way that means you didn't have criminal intent - like taking someone else's identical coat by accident
Elements that must be proven:
  • Honest mistake about facts
  • Mistake negates required mental state
  • Reasonable mistake (for negligence crimes)
  • Any mistake (for specific intent crimes)

TORTS LAW

Intentional Torts

Assault

Making someone reasonably afraid that you're about to hurt or offensively touch them - like drawing back your fist to punch
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause contact or apprehension
  • Reasonable apprehension of imminent contact
  • No consent

Battery

Actually touching someone in a harmful or offensive way on purpose - from a punch to spitting on someone
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to cause harmful/offensive contact
  • Harmful or offensive contact
  • Lack of consent

False Imprisonment

Trapping someone in a space and not letting them leave - like locking them in a room or blocking their only exit
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to confine
  • Actual confinement
  • Awareness of confinement or harm
  • No reasonable means of escape

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Doing something so horrible and outrageous that it severely traumatizes someone emotionally - behavior that "shocks the conscience"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Extreme and outrageous conduct
  • Intent or recklessness
  • Severe emotional distress
  • Causation

Trespass to Land

Going onto someone else's property without permission - even just stepping one foot over the property line counts
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to enter land
  • Entry onto land
  • Land in possession of another
  • No consent or privilege

Conversion

Messing with someone else's stuff so badly that you have to pay them the full value - like totaling their car or selling their bike
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to exercise dominion/control
  • Serious interference with chattel
  • Chattel belongs to another

Trespass to Chattel

Messing with someone else's personal property in a way that damages it or keeps them from using it temporarily
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intent to interfere with chattel
  • Actual interference with chattel
  • Chattel belongs to another
  • Harm to chattel or possessor

Defenses to Intentional Torts

Consent

Agreeing to let someone do something that would normally be wrong - like agreeing to get tackled in football
Elements that must be proven:
  • Voluntary agreement
  • Capacity to consent
  • Knowledge of nature of act
  • No fraud or duress

Self-Defense

Fighting back to protect yourself from being hurt, but only using as much force as you reasonably need
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief in need for protection
  • Imminent threat
  • Force proportionate to threat
  • No excessive force

Defense of Others

Using force to protect someone else from being hurt - different rules in different states about when this is allowed
Approaches:
  • Step-in-Shoes (limited to third party's right to self-defense)
  • Reasonable Appearances (based on defender's reasonable belief)

Defense of Property

Using reasonable force to protect your stuff from being stolen or damaged - but you can't kill someone over property
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief property threatened
  • Request to desist (when feasible)
  • No deadly force for personal property
  • Force reasonably necessary

Prevention of Crime

Using reasonable force to stop someone from committing a crime against others - more force allowed for felonies than misdemeanors
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable belief crime being committed
  • Force necessary to prevent crime
  • Force proportionate to crime threatened
  • No deadly force for misdemeanors

Recapture of Property

Getting your stolen property back using reasonable force - but you must act promptly and can't use deadly force

Re-entry Upon Land

Taking back your land that someone is wrongfully occupying - but you must act quickly and can't breach the peace
Elements that must be proven:
  • Wrongful dispossession
  • Fresh pursuit (prompt action)
  • No breach of the peace
  • Reasonable force only

Recapture of Chattel

Taking back your personal property from someone who wrongfully has it - like getting your stolen bike back
Elements that must be proven:
  • Rightful possession of property
  • Fresh pursuit or prompt demand
  • Reasonable force only
  • No deadly force

Shopkeeper's Rule

Store owners can detain suspected shoplifters for reasonable investigation using reasonable force for reasonable time
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable suspicion of theft
  • Reasonable manner of detention
  • Reasonable time period
  • For reasonable investigation only

Legal Authority

Acting under valid legal authority - like police officers making arrests or security guards detaining suspects
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid legal authority
  • Acting within scope of authority
  • Reasonable force under circumstances

Necessity

Being allowed to damage someone's property to prevent something much worse - like breaking down a door to escape a fire
Types:
  • Public Necessity (complete defense, no liability)
  • Private Necessity (limited defense, liable for actual harm)

Negligence

Negligence

Not being as careful as a reasonable person would be, and someone gets hurt because of your carelessness
Elements that must be proven:
  • Duty of care
  • Breach of duty
  • Causation (actual and proximate)
  • Damages

Duty

Having a legal responsibility to be careful around other people - usually based on how foreseeable harm would be
General duty:
  • Foreseeability of harm
  • Relationship between parties
  • Policy considerations

Negligence Per Se

Breaking a safety law automatically counts as being negligent - like speeding in a school zone when a kid gets hit
Elements that must be proven:
  • Violation of safety statute
  • Plaintiff in protected class
  • Harm of type statute designed to prevent
  • No excuse for violation

Special Duty Categories

Certain relationships where you have to be extra careful - like doctors with patients or store owners with customers

Drivers of Vehicles

Drivers must use reasonable care under the circumstances - higher standard in emergency situations
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable care under driving conditions
  • Adherence to traffic laws
  • Attention to road conditions

Omission (Duty to Act)

Generally no duty to rescue, but exceptions exist - like if you created the danger or have a special relationship
Exceptions (duty exists):
  • Special relationship (parent-child, innkeeper-guest)
  • Created the peril
  • Undertook to help (Good Samaritan)
  • Statutory duty

Good Samaritans / Rescuers

Once you start helping someone in danger, you must use reasonable care - but you're not liable for making things worse than they were
Elements that must be proven:
  • Undertaking to render aid
  • Reasonable care in performance
  • No liability for failing to improve situation
  • Liability only if making situation worse

Negligent Supervision

Failing to properly supervise people under your care - like parents with children or employers with dangerous employees
Elements that must be proven:
  • Supervisory relationship
  • Knowledge of dangerous propensities
  • Failure to exercise reasonable supervision
  • Harm caused by supervised person

Professionals

Doctors, lawyers, and other professionals must meet the standard of their profession - what would a reasonable professional do?
Elements that must be proven:
  • Professional relationship
  • Standard of reasonable professional
  • Deviation from professional standard
  • Expert testimony usually required

Land Occupiers

Different levels of care owed to different types of people who come onto your property - from trespassers to invited guests
Traditional categories:
  • Trespasser - duty to avoid willful/wanton harm
  • Licensee - duty to warn of known dangers
  • Invitee - duty of reasonable care, inspect for dangers

Lessors & Lessees

Landlords generally not liable for tenant injuries, but exceptions exist for common areas and known dangerous conditions
Landlord liable when:
  • Common areas under landlord control
  • Known dangerous conditions not disclosed
  • Covenant to repair
  • Public use exception

Breach

Not being as careful as you should have been - failing to meet the "reasonable person" standard
Elements that must be proven:
  • Standard of care (reasonable person)
  • Deviation from standard

Actual Causation

Proving that your carelessness actually caused the harm - "but for" your actions, would the person still be hurt?
Elements that must be proven:
  • "But for" causation OR
  • Substantial factor test

Proximate Causation

Even if you caused harm, you're only responsible if it was the kind of harm that was reasonably foreseeable
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeability of harm
  • Direct causation OR
  • No superseding intervening cause

Watch the Time Line!

Important reminder: analyze the sequence of events carefully to determine if intervening causes break the chain of causation

Direct Causation

When defendant's negligent act directly causes plaintiff's harm without any intervening forces
Elements that must be proven:
  • Unbroken chain of causation
  • No intervening forces
  • Foreseeable type of harm

Intervening Acts

Forces that come between defendant's negligent act and plaintiff's harm - may or may not break the chain of causation

Independent Intervening Acts

Intervening forces that operate independently of defendant's negligence - more likely to be superseding and cut off liability
Examples:
  • Acts of God (earthquakes, lightning)
  • Criminal acts by third parties
  • Unforeseeable accidents
  • Intentional torts by third parties

Dependent Intervening Acts

Intervening forces that are triggered or made more likely by defendant's negligence - less likely to be superseding
Examples:
  • Negligent medical treatment
  • Rescue attempts
  • Foreseeable reactions by others
  • Subsequent accidents caused by original negligence

Res Ipsa Loquitur

"The thing speaks for itself" - when something bad happens that usually only occurs due to negligence, like surgical tools left inside patients
Elements that must be proven:
  • Event ordinarily doesn't occur without negligence
  • Instrumentality under defendant's exclusive control
  • No contributory negligence by plaintiff

Damages

What kind of harm you can recover for in negligence - generally requires physical injury, not just emotional harm

Physical Harm Needed

Traditional rule that you must suffer physical injury or property damage to recover in negligence - pure economic loss usually not recoverable
Elements that must be proven:
  • Physical injury to person OR
  • Physical damage to property
  • Economic loss tied to physical harm
  • No pure economic loss recovery

NIED (Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress)

Recovery for emotional harm caused by negligence - very limited, usually requires physical symptoms or being in zone of danger
Elements that must be proven:
  • Severe emotional distress
  • Zone of danger OR bystander recovery
  • Physical symptoms (traditional rule)
  • Reasonable foreseeability of distress

Negligence Defenses

Contributory Negligence

When you were also being careless and that helped cause your own injury - in some states, you get nothing
Elements that must be proven:
  • Plaintiff's unreasonable conduct
  • Contributing cause to harm
  • Failure to protect own safety

Comparative Negligence

Your damages get reduced based on how much the accident was your own fault - like 70% their fault, 30% yours
Types:
  • Pure Comparative (damages reduced by any % fault)
  • Modified Comparative (no recovery if plaintiff >50% at fault)

Last Clear Chance

Even if you were being careless, if the other person could have easily avoided hurting you at the last second, they're still liable
Elements that must be proven:
  • Plaintiff in position of helpless peril
  • Defendant aware of plaintiff's peril
  • Defendant had opportunity to avoid harm
  • Defendant failed to use reasonable care

Assumption of Risk

You can't sue for injuries from risks you knew about and voluntarily took - like getting hurt in a boxing match
Elements that must be proven:
  • Knowledge of specific risk
  • Voluntary assumption of risk
  • Risk materialized and caused harm

Multiple Defendant Issues

When multiple people cause injury, rules for dividing up liability and making sure the victim gets compensated

Joint, Concurrent, and Successive Liability

Different ways multiple defendants can be liable - acting together, acting separately at same time, or one after another
Types:
  • Joint - defendants acting in concert
  • Concurrent - independent acts causing indivisible harm
  • Successive - separate acts causing cumulative harm

Joint & Several Liability

When multiple defendants are liable, each can be held responsible for the entire damages - victim can collect full amount from any defendant
Elements that must be proven:
  • Multiple tortfeasors
  • Indivisible harm
  • Each defendant liable for whole amount
  • Victim recovers only once

Contribution & Indemnity

How defendants who pay more than their fair share can get money back from other responsible parties
Contribution:
  • Right to partial reimbursement
  • Based on comparative fault
  • Among joint tortfeasors
Indemnity:
  • Right to full reimbursement
  • From more culpable defendant
  • Vicarious liability situations

Strict Liability

Animals

Owner strict liability for harm caused by animals - different rules for wild animals versus domestic animals
Wild Animals:
  • Strict liability for any harm caused
  • No matter how careful owner was
  • Includes animals with dangerous propensities
Domestic Animals:
  • Strict liability only if owner knows of dangerous propensities
  • "One bite rule" for dogs
  • Trespassing animals - strict liability for property damage

Abnormally Dangerous Activities

Being responsible for harm even if you weren't careless - applies to ultra-dangerous activities and defective products
Elements that must be proven:
  • High degree of risk
  • Cannot be eliminated by reasonable care
  • Not common usage
  • Inappropriate to location
  • Danger outweighs value to community

Products Liability

Manufacturing Defect

When one particular product comes out wrong from the factory - like a car with brakes that don't work
Elements that must be proven:
  • Product differs from intended design
  • Defect existed when product left defendant
  • Defect caused plaintiff's injury
  • Defendant in commercial chain of distribution

Design Defect

When the whole product line is dangerous because of how it was designed - like a car that flips over easily
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeable risks of harm
  • Risk could have been reduced by reasonable alternative design
  • Alternative design's benefits outweigh costs
  • Design defect caused harm

Warning Defect

Not warning people about dangers they wouldn't know about - like not telling people a medicine causes drowsiness
Elements that must be proven:
  • Foreseeable risks of harm
  • Risk could be reduced by warnings/instructions
  • Failure to provide adequate warnings
  • Lack of warning caused harm

General Rule

One who sells any product in defective condition unreasonably dangerous is liable for harm caused to users/consumers
Requirements:
  • Seller must be engaged in business of selling
  • Product must reach user without substantial change
  • User/consumer or property must be harmed
  • No requirement of privity of contract

Theory

Products liability can be based on multiple legal theories depending on the circumstances and jurisdiction
Four Main Approaches:
  • Strict Liability in Tort (most common)
  • Negligence (failure to use reasonable care)
  • Warranty (express or implied)
  • Misrepresentation (false statements about product)

Vicarious Liability

Respondeat Superior

When your employee hurts someone while doing their job, you as the boss can be sued too - "let the master answer"
Elements that must be proven:
  • Employer-employee relationship
  • Employee acting within scope of employment
  • Tort committed by employee
  • Tort occurred during work time/place

Independent Contractor

Usually you're not responsible when an independent contractor hurts someone, but there are some important exceptions
Exceptions (when liability exists):
  • Non-delegable duty
  • Inherently dangerous activity
  • Apparent authority
  • Negligent selection/supervision

Joint Enterprise

When people are working together on something and all have equal control, they can all be liable for each other's mistakes
Elements that must be proven:
  • Agreement to participate in common purpose
  • Common pecuniary interest
  • Equal right of control
  • Tort by one participant

To/From Home

When employees are traveling to or from their normal workplace, they're usually not considered "on the job" - but there are exceptions
Key Rules:
  • General Rule: No liability for commute
  • Special Mission Exception: If running employer errand
  • Tools/Equipment Exception: Carrying employer property
  • Zone of Employment: Still on employer's premises

Frolic and Detour

When employees go off on personal business during work time - slight detours might still be "on the job," major ones aren't
Analysis Framework:
  • Frolic: Major departure = no liability
  • Detour: Minor departure = possible liability
  • Consider: Time, place, purpose, extent of deviation
  • Re-entry: When employee returns to work duties

Automobile Owner Liability

If you let someone borrow your car and they cause an accident, you might be liable too depending on your state's laws

Family Purpose Doctrine

Car owner liable when family members use the family car for their own purposes - treats car as family instrumentality
Elements that must be proven:
  • Car maintained for family use
  • Family member driving with permission
  • Use for family purpose
  • Negligent driving by family member

Consent Statutes

State laws that make car owners liable for accidents caused by anyone driving with the owner's permission
Elements that must be proven:
  • Owner gave permission to use vehicle
  • Use within scope of permission
  • Negligent driving by permittee
  • Causal connection to harm

Bailor / Bailee

When you temporarily give your property to someone else to hold - liability depends on who benefits from the arrangement
Types of bailment:
  • For sole benefit of bailor - slight care required
  • Mutual benefit - reasonable care required
  • For sole benefit of bailee - great care required

Parent / Child

Parents generally not liable for their children's torts, but exceptions exist for negligent supervision or family purpose doctrine
Parent liable when:
  • Negligent supervision
  • Entrusted child with dangerous instrumentality
  • Child acting as parent's agent
  • Statutory liability (some states)

Business Torts

Interference with Contract

Intentionally ruining someone else's existing business deal - like convincing someone to break their contract with a competitor
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid contract between plaintiff and third party
  • Defendant's knowledge of contract
  • Intentional interference
  • Breach of contract
  • Damages to plaintiff

Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

Sabotaging someone's future business opportunities that weren't locked down in a contract yet
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonably probable future economic benefit
  • Defendant's knowledge of relationship
  • Intentional interference
  • Loss of prospective benefit
  • Wrongful means or lack of justification

Disparagement

False statements that hurt someone's business or property value - like saying a restaurant has rats when it doesn't
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement about plaintiff's business/property
  • Publication to third parties
  • Intent to harm or reckless disregard
  • Special damages (specific economic loss)
  • Causation between statement and loss

Crossover Torts

Misrepresentation

Giving false information that causes someone to act and get hurt financially - can be intentional, negligent, or innocent
Intentional Misrepresentation (Fraud)
Deliberately lying to someone to trick them into doing something that hurts them financially
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement of material fact
  • Knowledge of falsity (scienter)
  • Intent to induce reliance
  • Justifiable reliance by plaintiff
  • Damages resulting from reliance
Negligent Misrepresentation
Carelessly giving wrong information when you should have been more careful, causing financial harm to someone who relied on it
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement of material fact
  • Failure to exercise reasonable care
  • Pecuniary interest or business/professional relationship
  • Justifiable reliance by plaintiff
  • Economic damages resulting from reliance
Innocent Misrepresentation
Giving false information without knowing it's false and without being careless - still liable if it causes financial harm
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement of material fact
  • No knowledge of falsity
  • Justifiable reliance by plaintiff
  • Financial loss from the transaction
  • Restitution as primary remedy

Wrongful Litigation

Using the legal system improperly to hurt someone - includes both starting bad lawsuits and wrongfully continuing them
Malicious Prosecution
Starting a criminal case against someone without probable cause and for bad reasons
Elements that must be proven:
  • Defendant initiated criminal proceedings
  • Proceedings terminated in plaintiff's favor
  • Lack of probable cause
  • Malicious purpose or improper motive
  • Damages to plaintiff
Abuse of Process
Using legal proceedings properly started but for an improper purpose - like using a valid lawsuit to extort someone
Elements that must be proven:
  • Use of legal process
  • Primarily for ulterior purpose
  • Purpose different from what process was designed for
  • Damages to plaintiff
  • Willful act in use of process

Defamation

Defamation

Spreading false information that damages someone's reputation - includes both spoken (slander) and written (libel) lies
Elements that must be proven:
  • False statement of fact
  • Publication to third party
  • Damages to reputation
  • Fault (negligence for private figures, actual malice for public figures)

Libel

Written lies about someone that hurt their reputation - like false newspaper articles or social media posts

Slander

Spoken lies about someone that hurt their reputation - like spreading false rumors or gossip
Slander per se (no damages required):
  • Accuses of crime involving moral turpitude
  • Accuses of loathsome disease
  • Imputes unchastity to woman
  • Affects business/professional reputation

Privileges (Defenses to Defamation)

Special protections that allow people to make statements that might otherwise be defamatory - based on importance of free speech in certain contexts

Absolute Privileges

Complete immunity from defamation suits - even if statement was made with actual malice
Contexts with absolute privilege:
  • Judicial proceedings (judges, lawyers, parties, witnesses)
  • Legislative proceedings (legislators in official capacity)
  • Executive/administrative proceedings (high officials)
  • Spousal communications

Qualified/Conditional Privileges

Protection from defamation suits unless the speaker acted with malice or exceeded the scope of privilege
Contexts with qualified privilege:
  • Public interest (warnings about public danger)
  • Common interest (statements to people with shared concern)
  • Self-defense (responding to attacks on reputation)
  • Employment references (good faith recommendations)

Invasion of Privacy

Intrusion Upon Seclusion

Invading someone's privacy in a way that would really offend a reasonable person - like secretly recording them in their bedroom
Elements that must be proven:
  • Intentional intrusion
  • Private place/matter
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person

Public Disclosure of Private Facts

Spreading true but private information about someone that would embarrass them and isn't the public's business
Elements that must be proven:
  • Publication of private facts
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person
  • Not of legitimate public concern

Appropriation of Likeness

Using someone's name or picture to make money without their permission - like putting their face on products to sell them
Elements that must be proven:
  • Use of plaintiff's name or likeness
  • For commercial purposes
  • Without plaintiff's consent
  • Damages to plaintiff

False Light

Publishing information that makes someone look bad in a way that's false and highly offensive - similar to defamation
Elements that must be proven:
  • Publication of material concerning plaintiff
  • Places plaintiff in false light
  • Highly offensive to reasonable person
  • Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard

Damages

Special Damages

Money damages you can calculate exactly - like medical bills, lost wages, or repair costs

General Damages

Money for pain, suffering, and emotional distress - harder to calculate than special damages

Punitive Damages

Extra money awarded to punish someone for being especially bad and to deter others from similar behavior
Elements that must be proven:
  • Willful, wanton, or malicious conduct
  • Clear and convincing evidence
  • Reprehensible conduct
  • Constitutional limits on amount

Avoidable Consequence Rule

Victims must take reasonable steps to minimize their damages after being injured - can't recover for harm they could have prevented
Elements that must be proven:
  • Reasonable opportunity to mitigate
  • Failure to take reasonable steps
  • Additional harm was avoidable

Collateral Source Rule

Benefits received from other sources (like insurance or charity) don't reduce the defendant's liability - defendant pays full damages
Elements that must be proven:
  • Benefit from independent source
  • Source not connected to defendant
  • Policy supports non-reduction of damages

Miscellaneous Tort Concepts

Wrongful Death

When someone dies because of another person's wrongful actions, their family can sue for money damages
Elements that must be proven:
  • Death of person
  • Caused by defendant's tort
  • Statutory beneficiary bringing suit
  • Pecuniary loss to beneficiaries

Survival Action

A lawsuit that a dead person could have filed while alive can still be filed by their estate after they die
Elements that must be proven:
  • Valid tort claim by decedent
  • Claim accrued before death
  • Proper representative bringing suit
  • Damages from tort to decedent

Statute of Limitations

Time limits for filing lawsuits - if you wait too long, you lose your right to sue
Elements that must be proven:
  • Time limit has passed
  • Claim accrued before statute ran
  • No tolling or extension

Immunities

Special protections that prevent certain people from being sued in tort - based on relationships or official positions

Husband/Wife (Interspousal Immunity)

Traditional rule that spouses couldn't sue each other in tort - largely abolished in modern times
Modern trend:
  • Most states have abolished immunity
  • Spouses can sue for intentional torts
  • Some limitations remain for negligence

Parent/Child (Intrafamily Immunity)

Traditional rule that family members couldn't sue each other - partially abolished, varies by state
Modern trend:
  • Children can sue parents for intentional torts
  • Limited immunity for parental discipline
  • Some states allow negligence suits
  • Emancipated minors treated as adults

Charities (Charitable Immunity)

Traditional rule that charitable organizations couldn't be sued - largely abolished in modern times
Elements:
  • Most states have abolished immunity
  • Some limited immunity for volunteers
  • Insurance requirements in many states

Government (Sovereign Immunity)

Government traditionally immune from tort suits - partially waived by tort claims acts with many exceptions
Exceptions (immunity retained):
  • Discretionary functions
  • Legislative and judicial functions
  • Military activities
  • Some intentional torts by employees

Nuisance

When someone's activities substantially interfere with your use and enjoyment of your property - like loud noise or bad smells
Elements that must be proven:
  • Substantial interference
  • Unreasonable interference
  • With use and enjoyment of land
  • Causation by defendant's conduct
